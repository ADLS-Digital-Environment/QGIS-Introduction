A refresher on Coordinate Reference Systems and Projections

WGS84 is a Geographic Coordinate System
Geographic Coordinate Systems

  • They see the world as a sphere
  • They use longitude and latitude
  • They are useful for global data
  • They are cumbersome for small-scale applications
  • The most important is WGS84
WGS84:

  • EPSG Code: 4326 (!!!)
  • West-East -180 to +180
  • South-North -90 to +90
  • Switzerland
    • West-East 5.9 - 10.4
    • South-North 45.8 - 47.8
Projected Coordinate Systems

  • They abstract the world to a 2D-plane
  • They use x/y or E/N coordinates
  • They are useful for local (e.g. National) data
  • Cannot be used for global data
  • The most important (for us) are CH1903+LV95 and CH1903 LV03

  • EPSG Code: 2056
  • is the new swiss coordinate system
  • is centered on Bern
  • West-East 2.4-2.8m
  • South-North 1.1-1.3m

    Let's calculate the normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI).

    ... a simple indicator that can be used to analyze remote sensing measurements, often from a space platform, assessing whether or not the target being observed contains live green vegetation.
    \[\begin{aligned} \text{NDVI}& = \frac{\text{NIR}-\text{Red}}{\text{NIR}+\text{Red}} \\ \end{aligned} \]
    Near Infrared (NIR) Band 8
    Red (R) Band 4
    • Import the bands 8 (nir) and 4 (red)
    • Rename the bands to their respective names (NIR and Red)
    • Open the Raster calculator
    • Input the Formula
    • Choose a Reference Layer (to automatically asign Cellsize, CRS and Extent)
    • Run function and visualize with singleband pseudocolour ranging from -1 to 1, linear interpolation, red to green and try different modes (continuous, quantile)
